% ─── ABSTRACT ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We propose a multi-scale regime detection framework that fuses four
complementary detectors---CUSUM sequential analysis on z-score
returns, EWMA volatility crossover, Hamilton Markov-switching with a
recursive out-of-sample filter, and PELT structural break
detection---into a unified stress probability via Takagi-Sugeno fuzzy
inference with bounded L-BFGS-B optimization.  The composite regime
signal drives an adaptive sector portfolio strategy that classifies
eleven S\&P~500 sector ETFs into three baskets (Tactical, Avoid, Core)
based on GARCH(1,1)-$t$ conditional volatility terciles and
Ornstein-Uhlenbeck recovery half-life, with implicit cash allocation
as the primary risk-reduction mechanism.  In a walk-forward backtest
spanning 2009--2025 with quarterly recalibration, the framework
achieves a Sharpe ratio of 0.78, a maximum drawdown of $-19.60\%$
(versus $-41.71\%$ for SPY), and a Calmar ratio of 0.59, while
capturing 98\% of the benchmark return at 73\% of its volatility.
All thresholds and parameters are estimated from data using documented
statistical methods, with zero lookahead bias enforced throughout.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} regime detection, fuzzy inference, portfolio management, CUSUM, Markov switching, GARCH, walk-forward validation, implicit cash allocation
\end{abstract}

% ─── INTRODUCTION ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

Financial markets exhibit regime-dependent behavior characterized by
distinct periods of calm, transition, and stress
\citep{hamilton1989}.  Traditional portfolio strategies that assume
stationary return dynamics suffer significant drawdowns during regime
shifts, as the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, the 2020 COVID-19 crash,
and the 2022 interest rate hiking cycle demonstrated.  Even
diversified portfolios that hold all sectors with equal or
risk-proportional weights cannot avoid the synchronous drawdowns
that characterize systemic stress events.

The central challenge in regime-adaptive portfolio management is
\emph{timely} and \emph{reliable} regime detection.  Individual
detectors operate on different time-scales and suffer from distinct
failure modes: short-term indicators produce false alarms during
idiosyncratic volatility, while long-term models react too slowly to
protect against rapid drawdowns.  The secondary challenge is
translating regime signals into portfolio actions that actually reduce
risk: a system that detects stress but merely redistributes weight
among risky assets---rather than moving to cash---provides limited
drawdown protection.

This paper addresses both challenges.  We propose an ensemble
architecture that fuses four complementary detectors through a
Takagi-Sugeno fuzzy inference system \citep{takagi1985}, combined with
an adaptive basket allocation framework where implicit cash allocation
serves as the primary risk-reduction mechanism.

Our contributions are fourfold:
\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=2pt]
    \item A multi-scale detector ensemble spanning ultra-short (5-day)
          to long-term (252+ day) horizons, with CUSUM operating on
          z-score-standardized returns for scale invariance and
          Markov-switching using a recursive Hamilton filter for
          real-time out-of-sample updates.
    \item A fuzzy aggregation framework that learns optimal detector
          weights and membership functions by minimizing the Brier
          score against realized drawdowns, with bounded optimization
          and minimum weight floors to prevent detector suppression.
    \item A tercile-based basket construction methodology that
          classifies sector ETFs using GARCH(1,1)-$t$ conditional
          volatility and Ornstein-Uhlenbeck recovery dynamics, with
          graduated Core-basket response to extreme stress.
    \item An implicit cash mechanism where stress-induced weight
          reduction produces sub-unity portfolio allocation,
          with the deficit earning the risk-free rate rather than
          being redistributed to other risky assets.
\end{enumerate}

% ─── RELATED WORK ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}

\citet{hamilton1989} introduced Markov-switching models for business
cycle analysis, establishing the foundation for regime-based asset
allocation.  \citet{ang2002} extended this framework to international
asset allocation, demonstrating that regime-switching models can
improve out-of-sample portfolio performance when properly calibrated.

\citet{gradojevic2013} demonstrated the effectiveness of fuzzy logic
systems for financial decision-making under uncertainty.  The
Takagi-Sugeno framework \citep{takagi1985} provides a principled
approach to combining multiple information sources with calibratable
membership functions.  Our work extends this by jointly optimizing
membership parameters and detector weights with explicit bounds to
prevent degenerate solutions.

\citet{nystrup2017} showed that regime-based asset allocation
outperforms static strategies when the regime model is properly
calibrated.  \citet{kirby2012} demonstrated that simple timing
strategies based on inverse-volatility weighting outperform na\"ive
diversification.  Our framework builds on inverse-volatility weighting
as the base allocation and adds regime-dependent scaling with an
implicit cash buffer.

\citet{page1954} introduced the CUSUM procedure for sequential change
detection.  \citet{killick2012} proposed the PELT algorithm for
optimal change-point detection with linear computational cost.
\citet{siegmund1985} derived average run length approximations for setting CUSUM
decision thresholds.  We apply CUSUM to z-score-standardized returns
to achieve scale-invariant detection thresholds.

% ─── METHODOLOGY ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Multi-Scale Detector Ensemble}

We deploy four detectors targeting complementary time-scales (Figure~\ref{fig:detector_covid} illustrates their coordinated response during the COVID-19 crash).  All parameters are estimated from training data at each walk-forward step.

\subsubsection{CUSUM Sequential Detector (5--10 day)}
Following \citet{page1954}, we maintain upward and downward CUSUM
accumulators on \emph{z-score-standardized} returns:
\begin{equation}
    z_t = \frac{r_t - \hat{\mu}_0}{\hat{\sigma}_{\text{train}}}
\end{equation}
\begin{align}
    S_t^+ &= \max(0,\; S_{t-1}^+ + z_t - k) \\
    S_t^- &= \max(0,\; S_{t-1}^- - z_t - k)
\end{align}
where $\hat{\mu}_0$ and $\hat{\sigma}_{\text{train}}$ are the mean and
standard deviation of training-window returns, $k = 0.5$ (detecting a
1$\sigma$ shift in standardized space), and the decision threshold is
$h = (\ln(\text{ARL}_0) + 1.166) / \delta$ with $\delta = 1$ per
\citet{siegmund1985}, yielding $h \approx 6.70$ for a target average
run length of 252 trading days.  The signal is
$\min(\max(S_t^+, S_t^-)/h,\; 1)$.

Operating in z-score space makes the threshold \emph{scale-invariant}: a
single $-5\%$ daily return (approximately $z = -4.2$) produces a signal
of $\sim\!0.55$, while three consecutive $-2\%$ days ($z \approx -1.7$
each) yield a signal of $\sim\!0.54$.  Under the original raw-return
formulation, the threshold was approximately 558, requiring over
12{,}000 consecutive crash days to trigger---rendering the detector
inoperative.

\subsubsection{EWMA Volatility Crossover (21--42 day)}
Two EWMA volatility estimators with decay factors
$\lambda_{\text{fast}}$ and $\lambda_{\text{slow}}$ are calibrated via
maximum likelihood estimation \citep{longerstaey1996}:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\sigma}^2_{\cdot,t} = \lambda_\cdot\, \hat{\sigma}^2_{\cdot,t-1}
                       + (1 - \lambda_\cdot)\, r_t^2
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\text{fast}} \in [0.85, 0.96]$ and
$\lambda_{\text{slow}} \in [0.95, 0.995]$.  The crossover ratio
$(\hat{\sigma}_{\text{fast}} - \hat{\sigma}_{\text{slow}}) /
\hat{\sigma}_{\text{slow}}$ is normalized to $[0,1]$ via the empirical
CDF of training-window values.

\subsubsection{Hamilton Markov-Switching with Recursive Filter (63--126 day)}
We fit a 2-state Markov-switching regression \citep{hamilton1989} on
weekly SPY returns:
\begin{equation}
    r_t \mid S_t = i \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}(\mu_i, \sigma_i^2),
    \quad i \in \{0, 1\}
\end{equation}
During the out-of-sample window, rather than holding the filtered
probability constant, we implement a \emph{recursive Hamilton filter
update}.  Daily returns are accumulated into a pseudo-weekly buffer,
and every 5 trading days the filter is updated via the standard
predict--likelihood--update cycle:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\xi}_{t|t} =
    \frac{\boldsymbol{\xi}_{t|t-1} \odot \boldsymbol{\eta}}
         {\mathbf{1}^\top(\boldsymbol{\xi}_{t|t-1} \odot
         \boldsymbol{\eta})}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\xi}_{t|t-1} = \mathbf{P}^\top
\boldsymbol{\xi}_{t-1|t-1}$ is the prediction step using the
transition matrix $\mathbf{P}$, and $\eta_j = \phi(r_t^{(w)};\,
\mu_j, \sigma_j^2)$ are state-conditional likelihoods of the
pseudo-weekly return.  The signal is
$P(S_t = \text{stress} \mid r_1, \ldots, r_t)$.

\subsubsection{Structural Break Detection (252+ day)}
Using the PELT algorithm \citep{killick2012}, we detect structural
breaks in the cumulative return series with a BIC-calibrated penalty
$\lambda = \ln(n) \cdot \hat{\sigma}^2$.  The signal decays
exponentially from the most recent break:
$s_t = \exp(-d_t / \tau)$ where $d_t$ is the number of days since the
last break and $\tau = 21$.

\subsection{Fuzzy Aggregation Framework}

Each detector signal $x_i \in [0,1]$ passes through a calibrated
sigmoid membership function \citep{takagi1985}:
\begin{equation}
    \mu_{\text{stress}}(x_i) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(-a_i(x_i - c_i))}
\end{equation}
The composite stress probability is:
\begin{equation}
    P(\text{stress}) = \sum_{i=1}^{4} w_i \cdot
                       \mu_{\text{stress}}(x_i), \quad
                       \sum w_i = 1,\; w_i \geq w_{\min}
\end{equation}

Parameters $\{a_i, c_i, w_i\}$ are jointly optimized by minimizing the
Brier score \citep{brier1950}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{Brier}} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}
    \bigl(P_{\text{composite},t} - D_t\bigr)^2
\end{equation}
where $D_t = 1$ if a drawdown exceeding the 10th percentile of
training-window drawdowns occurs within the next 21 trading days.
Optimization uses L-BFGS-B with bounds: $a_i \in [1, 50]$,
$c_i \in [0.05, 0.95]$, raw weights $\tilde{w}_i \in [-5, 5]$
(mapped through softmax).  A minimum weight floor of
$w_{\min} = 0.05$ is applied:
\begin{equation}
    w_i = w_{\min} + (1 - 4 w_{\min}) \cdot
    \text{softmax}(\tilde{w})_i
\end{equation}
Figure~\ref{fig:membership} shows the calibrated membership functions and optimized detector weights.

\subsection{Asset Characterization and Basket Construction}

Each sector ETF is characterized by GARCH(1,1)-$t$ conditional
volatility \citep{bollerslev1986, engle1982} and Ornstein-Uhlenbeck
recovery half-life \citep{uhlenbeck1930}.  Figure~\ref{fig:scatter}
shows the resulting classification landscape.  Sectors are assigned to
three baskets using \emph{tercile} boundaries on conditional volatility
(33rd and 67th percentiles) combined with median half-life:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=4pt]
    \item \textbf{Basket~A (Tactical):} Top or middle tercile vol with
          fast recovery.  Binary liquidation above the calibrated
          entry threshold; re-entry below the exit threshold.
    \item \textbf{Basket~B (Avoid):} Top tercile vol with slow
          recovery.  Continuous de-risking:
          $w_i = w_i^{\text{base}} \cdot (1 - P(\text{stress}))$.
    \item \textbf{Basket~C (Core):} Bottom tercile vol.  Graduated
          linear scaling above $P(\text{stress}) = 0.7$:
          \begin{equation}
              w_i = w_i^{\text{base}} \cdot
              \max\!\left(0,\;
              \frac{1 - P(\text{stress})}{0.3}\right)
          \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implicit Cash Allocation}

Base weights are computed using inverse-volatility weighting
\citep{kirby2012}, \emph{normalized to sum to 1.0 before any stress
scaling is applied}.  After basket-specific stress scaling:
\begin{equation}
    W_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i} w_i \leq 1.0, \qquad
    w_{\text{cash}} = 1 - W_{\text{total}}
\end{equation}
The deficit earns the daily risk-free rate.  This design avoids a
critical pitfall: when stressed assets are de-weighted and renormalized
to sum to 1.0, the freed capacity is redistributed to remaining
assets, potentially \emph{increasing} concentration risk during stress.
By allowing sub-unity exposure, the system achieves genuine risk reduction.

\subsection{Walk-Forward Validation}

The system is validated using expanding-window walk-forward analysis
with a 504-day (2-year) minimum training window and 63-day (quarterly)
out-of-sample steps.  At each step, \emph{all} parameters are
re-estimated from training data only.  Transaction costs of 10 basis points
per trade are applied to all weight changes.

evaluation period covers 4{,}274 trading days from January 2009 to
December 2025.

% ─── DISCUSSION ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Normalization Problem}

A critical design insight is that portfolio weight normalization can
negate regime-responsive behavior.  Inverse-volatility weights for $N$
assets produce raw sums of $\sim$20--30.  When stressed assets are
de-weighted and the result is normalized to sum to 1.0, the freed
capacity is redistributed proportionally to surviving positions---including
Core assets.  This means the portfolio \emph{increases} its allocation
to these assets during stress, the opposite of risk reduction.  Our
approach normalizes base weights to 1.0 \emph{before} stress scaling,
so that post-scaling sums below 1.0 represent genuine cash allocation.

\subsection{Detector Scale Invariance}

The z-score standardization of CUSUM inputs is critical for practical
deployment.  With raw returns, the Siegmund threshold scales inversely
with the standard deviation ($h \propto 1/\sigma$), producing
thresholds of $\sim$558 for typical equity returns ($\sigma \approx 0.012$).
Daily accumulator increments of $\sim$0.04 require thousands of
consecutive crash days to trigger.  In z-score space, the threshold is
$\sim$6.70 regardless of return scale, and a single $-5\%$ day produces
a meaningful signal of 0.55.

\subsection{Limitations}

The framework has several limitations.  First, the annualized turnover
of 4.01$\times$ creates transaction cost drag, though the 10~bps assumption
is conservative for liquid ETFs.  Second, the quarterly recalibration frequency
may lag rapidly evolving dynamics; event-driven recalibration could improve
responsiveness.  Third, the CUSUM detector's $\sim$78\% weight dominance
suggests the remaining detectors contribute primarily through ensemble
diversity.  Fourth, the backtest spans a single macroeconomic epoch
(post-GFC monetary expansion); performance in structurally different
interest rate environments requires further validation.

\subsection{Robustness}

All parameters are re-estimated at each walk-forward step using only
past data.  The bounded L-BFGS-B optimization with minimum weight
floors prevents convergence to degenerate single-detector solutions.
The implicit cash mechanism provides a natural risk-reduction pathway
that does not depend on predicting the \emph{type} of stress, only its
\emph{presence}.

% ─── CONCLUSION ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}

We have presented a multi-scale regime detection framework that fuses
four complementary detectors through Takagi-Sugeno fuzzy inference for
adaptive sector portfolio management.  The walk-forward validated
results demonstrate a Sharpe ratio of 0.78 with a maximum drawdown of
$-$19.60\%---a 53\% reduction relative to the SPY benchmark---while
maintaining nearly identical annualized returns.

The key innovations are: (1)~z-score standardization of CUSUM inputs
for scale-invariant detection; (2)~recursive Hamilton filtering for
real-time Markov-switching updates; (3)~bounded fuzzy optimization with
minimum detector weight floors; and (4)~implicit cash allocation through
sub-unity weight sums rather than forced normalization.

The framework's success lies not in predicting market direction but in
reliably detecting the \emph{onset} of stress and responding with
genuine risk reduction.  During the COVID-19 crash, the portfolio moved
to 79\% cash within days of the drawdown onset and gradually re-entered
during recovery.  This asymmetric response---fast to de-risk, gradual
to re-risk---is the primary driver of the improved risk-adjusted
performance.

Future work may explore: (1)~event-driven recalibration triggered by
detector alerts; (2)~cross-sectional regime signals from equity factor
models; (3)~extension to multi-asset-class allocation; and
(4)~incorporation of options-based tail hedging as a complement to the
implicit cash mechanism.
